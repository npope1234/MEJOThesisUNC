More than an ecological problem

Across the Southeastern United States, invasive plants are often treated as a simple ecological problem: species spread, native plants decline and land managers respond. But this project asks readers to look deeper and view invasive plants as part of a more complex environmental and cultural story. Invasive plant management is also tied to histories of colonial plant movement, Indigenous land relationships, tribal stewardship, climate pressure and questions about who controls environmental data.

Beyond biology, the idea of ecological imperialism shows how colonial practices, trade, government programs and land management decisions helped move plants across borders and into new landscapes. Some species now labeled invasive were introduced intentionally for agriculture, erosion control, ornamental landscaping or economic use. Their spread continues to reshape forests, rivers, wetlands and communities, while also echoing longer histories of land dispossession and environmental change.

The language used to describe invasive species matters, too. National and regional coverage often focuses on threats, eradication and management, especially for familiar plants such as kudzu. Federal agencies, conservation organizations and researchers track invasive species through occurrence data, remote sensing and spatial analysis. While this manner is helpful, media coverage can also rely on alarmist language, incorrect imagery or simple “native versus invasive” narratives that leave out important context.

Indigenous scholars, activists and land stewards have challenged those simplified narratives. Their perspectives show that plant management is not only about removal. It can also involve kinship, responsibility, relationship-building, community consent and the protection of cultural knowledge. In some cases, non-native species may become part of local ecosystems or community practices, complicating the idea that every introduced plant should be understood in the same way.

This site follows connected paths: the plants reshaping Southeastern landscapes, the tribal land stewardship that challenges conventional conservation narratives, and the maps and data tools that can reveal patterns while also leaving important community context out.

The Plants +

Invasive plants are not simply “bad” plants. Many were introduced through ornamental horticulture, agriculture, erosion control and other human decisions. Experts like Alan Weakley, director of the UNC Herbarium, explain how species such as kudzu, privet, hydrilla and Japanese honeysuckle reshape forests, waterways and cultural landscapes across the Southeast.

Tribal Land Stewardship +

For Native communities, land is not just property or habitat. It is history, food, water, memory and responsibility. Accounts from Larry Edwards and Philip Bell, representatives of the Lumbee and Coharie tribes of North Carolina, provide an opportunity to center Indigenous voices and show how invasive plant management intersects with land access, tribal sovereignty and the authority to define environmental care.

Data Mapping +

Maps can reveal where invasive plants have been recorded, but they do not tell the whole story. Experts like Dr. Charles “Chuck” Bargeron, director of the University of Georgia’s Center for Invasive Species and Ecosystem Health, and Ming Shen, a geography researcher studying kudzu through remote sensing, explain how occurrence data, climate charts and satellite imagery can help track invasive plant spread. Dr. Danielle Hiraldo, director of the American Indian Center at UNC-Chapel Hill and a citizen of the Lumbee Tribe, adds another layer: mapping and environmental data must also account for community knowledge, consent and Indigenous data sovereignty.

Thesis Question

How do invasive plant species affect the biodiversity, local economies, and ecosystem health of modern day Indigenous lands, and how can centering Indigenous environmental perspectives challenge oversimplified media narratives of invasive plant species?

This platform aims to contribute new knowledge by interrogating the intersection of ecological imperialism, climate change, and Indigenous environmental stewardship. Rather than positioning invasive species as purely harmful, this research recognizes invasive species as complex ecological and cultural phenomena shaped by historical colonialism, environmental racism, and evolving climate patterns. By amplifying underrepresented Indigenous voices and knowledge systems (e.g., relational science and kinship traditions) this thesis challenges dominant environmental narratives and informs more nuanced, culturally responsive management strategies.
