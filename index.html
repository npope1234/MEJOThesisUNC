More than an ecological problem

Across the Southeastern United States, invasive plants are often treated as a simple ecological problem: species spread, native plants decline and land managers respond. But a closer look reveals a more complex situation. The story of invasive plants is one that encompasses culture, history, data, climate change, and the relationship between Indigenous peoples and the federal government.

Beyond biology, the idea of ecological imperialism shows how colonial practices, trade, government programs and land management decisions helped move plants across borders and into new landscapes. Some species now labeled invasive were introduced intentionally for agriculture, erosion control, ornamental landscaping or economic use. Their spread continues to reshape forests, rivers, wetlands and communities, while also echoing longer histories of land dispossession and environmental change.

The language used to describe invasive species matters, too. Media coverage often focuses on threats, eradication and management, especially for familiar plants such as kudzu. Stories about invasive plants also often feature alarmist language, incorrect imagery, and reporting that flattens a complex situation into a simple "native versus non-native" narrative.

Indigenous scholars, activists and land stewards have challenged those simplified narratives. Their perspectives show that plant management is not only about removal. It can also involve kinship, responsibility, relationship-building, community consent and the protection of cultural knowledge. In some cases, non-native species may become part of local ecosystems or community practices.

This project aims to educate the public about some of the most prolific invasive plants in the Southeast -- from well-known vines like kudzu to lesser-known species like alligator weed -- while also shedding light on the connection between these plants and the Native people with whom they share the landscape. It's a story about horticulture, data, and the conflict that often arises when people disagree about who gets to manage the land.
